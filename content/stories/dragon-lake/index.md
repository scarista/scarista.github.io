## I

Martha's eyes were red-rimmed from crying. She sat by the window of the bedroom she shared with her younger sister, trying not to sob too loudly. Her mother and father were whispering in the next room of their small cottage, and she did not wish to disturb them. She also wanted to make out what they were saying.

"So the elders have decided what they must do?" she heard her mother ask.

Her father grunted in acknowledgement. "They will send a rider to court. He will pass word to the Queen."

"But they have sent word twice before," her mother responded in disbelief. "It achieved nothing!"

"It is all they can do." Martha could picture her father's slow shrug, the wry smile on his face. "This is a village of farmers. We are not fighters. It will take soldiers to defeat the beast."

"And when will they send soldiers? When the beast lays waste to the castle gardens? When it takes up residence in the Royal chambers?"

There was a pause filled by the sound of father rising from his chair and crossing the hearth to mothers.

"Don't cry, my love. All will be well," he said soothingly.

"Oh, Martin, I'm sorry." Martha suppressed a sob at the sound of her mother's tears.

"Hush," said father. "Hush, my love."

Martha looked through the window at what remained of her father's fields. There would be no crop this year, the monster had seen to that with its fiery breath, leaving nothing but scorched stubble in its wake. She prayed next year would be different, but something father had said about the soil being poisoned by the beast made that seem unlikely.

Beyond father's land, she looked to the pasture where John Anders had kept his cattle. The monster had carried away the last of these only two nights before. Martha remembered how John had stood, fist raised to the sky, cursing the beast for all he was worth.

Throughout the valley, the story was the same. Every farm had suffered some loss because of the beast's hunger.

Martha rubbed one bleary eye with her sleeve and stifled another sob. The older children, whose stories she was not supposed to listen to, said that the monster was a dragon and that a dragon's "most favourite food was little children". She did not know whether to believe these stories, but she knew that, dragon or not, the beast was very real. Because of what it had done to the farms, she also understood that it was very bad. What she did not understand was why no one had stopped it.

She heard her father moving in the next room.

"It's time I got going, Alicia," he said to mother. "They'll be waiting down at the hall."

"Yes," she heard her mother say, "you go. I'll get supper ready and send Martha to collect Jayne. You won't be late?"

"No, there's not much to be said. Not since we sent the message to court. The allocations of what grain and salt meat we have in store are set. We need to check the remaining stocks of cheese and dried fruit and decide how to ration them, but that won't keep us long. Perhaps an hour."

There was a moment’s silence in which Martha knew her parents were exchanging a kiss.

"Until supper," her father said.

"Until supper," repeated mother.

She heard the front door of the cottage creak open. A moment later, it shut, and she heard the latch clank into place. She could not hear her mother's soft footfalls as she made her way to the stove, but the squeak of loose floorboards served instead.

"Martha!"

Her mother's shout swept away the last trace of her reverie about the dragon. As she had been taught, she hopped quickly from her bed and hurried to her mother's side.

"Yes, mother."

"I'm setting about the supper. Will you be a dear and fetch your sister?"

"Of course, mother."

"Good. And remember, no dallying."

With a shake of her head, she hurried to the rack by the front door, upon which hung a collection of capes and furs. She plucked her cape from its peg, set it about her shoul­ders, and deftly tied the thongs that held it in place.

"I'm going now, mother," she called over her shoulder.

"All right, dear. Hurry back so you can help cook."

Martha raised the latch and pulled the door open. She paused and took a deep breath of the cool, clean outside air before stepping across the porch and down the steps to the path below. As she skipped towards the hard-packed mud of the village road, she hummed a dancing tune her mother had taught her. Her earlier thoughts of the terrible dragon seemed very far away.

She stepped onto the road and stopped. What if, she thought, I could stop the dragon? She considered this idea for several seconds, then dismissed it as the childish nonsense her parents would tell her it was. Resuming her humming, she set off along the road to the cottage that was Lana Kieff's nursery.

Despite her dismissal, the thought that she might save the valley from the dragon stayed with her. What was more, the longer she thought about it, the more reasonable the idea seemed. Surely it could not be a difficult thing, to convince a dragon that what it was doing was wrong, that it was not right to destroy land and cattle. After all, the village dogs understood when they were scolded - could any other animal, even one as large as a dragon, be all that different?

The only answer she could put to that question was "No."

"I'll do it," she resolved as, looking up, she realised she had arrived, and that Jayne was calling to her from the doorway of Lana Kieff’s nursery.

## II

Martha could not eat her meal that evening. Her parents watched over her with concerned eyes, fearing she might be ill despite her reassurances to the contrary. When asked if she wished to retire early for some quiet rest, she declined.

"You should at least try a few mouthfuls, dear," her mother told her as she left the supper table.

"Sorry, mother, I just can't bring myself to eat."

"Well, I'll keep it warm lest you change your mind."

Martha nodded. "Thank you, mother. But I'm sure I won't."

This said, she hastened to her bedroom to make plans for her bold adventure.

The dragon's lair, she had heard elder villagers say, was in the foothills of the Harad Mountains, several miles to the north. She knew that the village road became the Harad Road once beyond the last of the valley's farms and that she could follow it right to the base of the mountains. The road did not actually enter the foothills, she remembered, rather it veered east along their base, entering the Harad Pass above the town of Malian. She would have to leave the road, find her own way into the hills and then on up to the dragon's lair. It would not be a simple journey but she would have to manage - the village, though it did not know it, was depending on her.

For the trip, she would need her warmest cape, her stout boots, a change of clothes, food, drink, and her satchel. The cape and boots sat by the front door so she could gather these as she left. She removed trousers, linen under things, a thick woollen pullover and socks and placed them in her satchel. She would have to sneak into the kitchen later and steal - the word made her cringe - some bread, cheese, and perhaps some of mother's sweet cakes. There, she told herself with some satisfaction, all that remains is to begin the journey.

Hours later, when midnight had come and gone, Martha rose from her bed and padded into the kitchen. Butterflies flut­tered in her stomach at the thought that her parents might waken and discover her purpose. She listened intently for any change in her father's snoring as it drifted from the depths of their room.

Moving as softly as any mouse, she took bread from the basket beside the stove. Then she crept into the larder and took cheese and a small skin of milk from the shaded cool-shelf, low against the outer wall. Her trembling heart nearly stopped when the lid of the sweet cake jar clinked as she lifted it. She remained as still as she could, hardly daring to breathe for almost a minute, certain one of her parents would awaken, discover, and scold her. When neither did, she scurried back to the haven of her room, provisions clutched tightly to her small breast.

Back in the bedroom, Jayne was a vague shape beneath blankets rising and falling to the slow rhythm of sleep. Aware that should she disturb her sister, she would raise a racket that would immediately rouse their parents, Martha packed quietly. Luck favoured her, however, and soon she was tiptoeing out of the bedroom and towards the front door.

After slipping into her boots and winding her cape about herself, she reached out to lift the door-latch.

"Oh," she exhaled softly. The door was locked, the key by her sleeping father's side. This was something she had not considered previously. How was she to begin her adventure now?

A window, she thought. I could wriggle out a window. But which one? Not in the bedroom because that would surely waken Jayne. The kitchen? No, as it was above the washtub and too difficult for her to reach. The larder? Yes, the larder window would be best. By standing on shelves, she could reach it easily. It was also on the far side of the cottage from her parent's room, so any noise she might make would be less likely to disturb them. She stole across to the larder and slipped inside.

It did not take her long to open the window, push her satchel through and then slip after it, closing the window gently behind herself. She crouched by the side of the cottage for several moments, making sure no one had been alerted to her actions. All remained quiet.

She scuttled down to the village road, where she stopped to look back at the cottage. Still, there was no sign of life. The thought of the warm bed she was leaving behind gave her cause to hesitate before her sense of duty to family and village reasserted itself. The dragon must be stopped, she told herself firmly. Without further pause, she turned and set forth along the road.

## III

Sunrise found her perched on a rock by the roadside, nibbling a chunk of bread. She marvelled at the crimson beauty of the early sky, completely unconcerned by her solitude. That she had just travelled four miles all alone and at night did not worry her either. It seemed such a natural thing to do whilst adventuring.

Deciding she'd had enough to eat, she replaced the bread in her satchel. She swallowed a little milk to wash her simple breakfast down, then hopped off the rock. There were many more miles to cover before she reached the Harad mountains so the sooner she was moving again the better.

In the distance ahead of her, she saw a black line stretching across half the horizon. Above this line loomed the purple-grey, snow-capped peaks of Harad. The black line, she surmised, must mark the eastern arm of the Varynym Forest.

A stretch of the road bisected the forest and she would have to traverse that stretch to reach the mountains. Though it would be dark beneath the boughs and the forest was the known haunt of thieves and vagabonds, she remained free from concern. All glorious adventures featured thieves and vagabonds, and forests populated with wonder. Why should either pose her any threat?

To the right of the road she espied a squat cube of stone. She stopped to inspect it.

"Malian, sixteen," she said aloud. "Harad Pass, twenty-one." That left another seven miles between her and the Harad foothills. "I wonder if I can reach them by nightfall," she murmured.

It then occurred to her that if she reached the foothills by dark, she would need a place to sleep. This thought gave her cause for some concern - she would have to sleep outside. However, her mind quickly returned to thoughts of adventurous deeds and she was comforted by the certainty that a cave, or abandoned cabin, would be found to fulfil her needs.

## IV

"Where is that child?" snapped Martha's mother as her father rose from the breakfast table.

"Out wandering, as she's done a hundred times before."

"And I've told her a hundred times never to sneak off without telling us where she's bound."

"I'm sure she'll be fine. There's probably some game being played somewhere we grown-ups aren't to know of. You know how youngsters are."

"Oh, yes, I know just how youngsters are." Now the concern in Alicia's voice was plain. "But they ought to know better. Especially with that accursed dragon about."

"I'm sure there's no danger from that beast," Martin said soothingly. "It raided barely three days ago and it'll now be sleeping soundly for another two. She'll be perfectly safe."

"But we don't know where she is, Martin. What if something happens and she can't get home? We won't know where to look for her."

Martin stepped to his wife's side and placed a comforting arm about her shoulders. "Let's leave it till I get back from pasture at midday. If there's no sign or word, I'll go looking for her. You can check with the other mothers when you take Jayne to Lana's. Maybe one of them will know something. All right?"

Alicia nodded, then turned her head to his shoulder, muffling a sob. He drew her closer for a moment, holding her tight. Then he raised her chin gently with his free hand.

"She'll be fine," he said softly as their gazes met. He placed a kiss on her forehead. "I'd best be off. You know what Peter and Abel are like, they'll not hang about if I'm late and I really need their help today."

"Yes," Alicia agreed. "Go quickly." She pecked his cheek. "Until midday."

"Until midday."

## V

By midday, the black line across the horizon had grown, its detail expanding so that Martha could now distinguish individual trees. A short while before, she passed another milestone and four more miles had fallen behind her. On passing the marker, she had decided not to stop for lunch until she reached the forest. Nature was softly calling to her, so she increased her pace to reach the trees sooner.

Fully an hour more went by before she stepped beneath the shade of the first tree. By this point, she felt fit to burst. After quickly checking to ensure there was no one about, she nipped off the road in search of a private spot.

When she was returning a few moments later, a sudden sound from among the trees startled her. She span towards the source of the noise in time to see a bush shake as from something’s passing.

"Who's there?" she hissed.

There was no reply. The bush moved again.

"Who's there?" she repeated, backing slowly away.

Now she discerned a shape within the bush. Something crouching. Something dark. She continued to back away, preparing herself to turn and run.

The bush moved a third time, this motion emphasised by a sudden yelp. With a squeal of surprise, Martha turned to run and tripped over her own feet. She landed in a breathless heap, looking back at the bush with wide eyes. At last, the foliage parted and revealed her tormentor.

A puppy? She giggled in her relief. The animal yapped at her, wagged its small tail, then cocked its head inquisitively. This afforded her a better opportunity to identify it.

"A wolf-cub?" she murmured and, after a moment of further consideration, she nodded slowly. It was indeed a wolf-cub.

With a sharp intake of breath, she stepped away from the cub, glancing about anxiously as she did so. Any instant now she was sure an enormous she-wolf would erupt from the foliage, desperate to protect her offspring.

When this failed to happen, Martha relaxed a little. The cub yapped again and crept closer to her. She looked down at it with tender eyes.

"Are you alone too?" she asked it softly.

The cub wagged its tail and gave issue to a full-throated bark, its wide eyes full of understanding.

Martha smiled. "Well, I'm on a quest to stop a dragon but, if that doesn't put you off, you can come with me."

Again, the bark. Again, the excited tail wagging. Then the cub trotted forward and sat directly before Martha, looking earnestly up at her. She crouched down and extended a tentative hand towards the cub. When it showed no sign of objecting, she gently stroked its head and tickled its ears. It returned the gesture, licking her hand with its rough, wet tongue.

"Okay, okay," giggled Martha. "I have to be going. Will you come with me?"

In answer, the cub dashed past her and up onto the road.

"Not so fast!" She hurried to catch up as the cub padded quickly along the road, through the forest and on towards the Harad foothills.

## VI

It had been past midday by the time Martin returned from his work in their pasture. Alicia had been frantic with worry on his arrival for all the other mothers had accounted for the whereabouts of their children and no one could say where they thought Martha might have gone.

"Be calm, Alicia." Martin spoke tenderly. "I'll go search for her. And I'm sure I won't have gone far before I find her."

"But no one has seen her. No one knows where she could be."

Martin reached up and gently dried his wife's eyes. "She'll be fine. If she's not in the village, she'll be off playing some game amidst the fields. That, or watching birds and animals and forgetting the time." His powerful arms enveloped Alicia and drew her close.

"Hush," he said, kissing her forehead.

Minutes later he was striding into the village, his intention to re-enlist the aid of Peter and Abel, the hands, for his hunt for his daughter.

"Martin!" John Anders hailed him from the roadside.

"I haven't time to talk, John," he called back without breaking his stride. "Martha's gone missing."

John stepped into Martin's wake and jogged to catch up with him. "Then you'll be looking for help in finding her," stated the big farmer.

"If you've the time, you're more than welcome."

"Where will you look for her?"

"She's likely to be hiding in one of the fields, or copses, watching the wildlife. I'm going to get Peter and Abel and have them look to the west of the village. We can do the east."

The other man nodded.

Peter and Abel were with the village clerk, discussing some minor repairs to the roof of the village hall. On hearing Martin's news, they were quick to agree to his plan for finding Martha.

After agreeing to meet back at the hall at dusk, whether or not they found the child, the four began the search.

To Martin's dismay, dusk arrived all too soon and he and John had found no trace of Martha.

"Perhaps Peter and Abel have had more luck." John Anders told his troubled friend.

"I hope so, John. I hope so. But, a feeling tells me they haven't."

"Such feelings are not always to be trusted. Besides, we may yet find her awaiting your return home."

Peter and Abel were waiting for them outside the hall and Martin's heart sank further when he saw Martha was not with them.

"Was there no sign of her?" he asked them.

Abel shook his head slowly. "I'm sorry, Martin, there was none."

"She may have made her own way home by now, though." Peter added, echoing John's earlier optimism.

"She may," agreed Martin, though he doubted as much. "Let's go there now."

"Pardon me?" The four men turned to face the speaker, a tall man whose garb and accent revealed him as a stranger.

"Yes, friend." Martin answered warmly despite his inner unease.

"Forgive me, but I could not help but overhear your conversation." He spoke directly to Martin. "Your daughter has gone missing?"

"Yes."

"Then perhaps I have news that may help you."

"How so?"

"I was gathering herbs on the fringe of the Varynym today and saw a lone child travelling the Harad Road."

"A girl?"

The stranger nodded. "A girl of some ten years, dressed in a bright blue cape, a leather satchel on her back."

"Martha! It must be! And she was travelling north?"

"Yes. And purposefully for one so young."

"Thank you, friend. Thank you." Martin was about to turn away, but he stopped himself. "Acht, look at me, forgetting myself. I'm sorry, I have not even asked your name."

The stranger raised a hand. "It is all right, given the circumstance under which we meet, I fully understand. My name is Malagar."

"Well, Malagar, my name is Martin and I am in your debt." He extended a hand, which Malagar clasped firmly. "These are John, Peter, and Abel." Each man shook Malagar's hand.

"Please," Martin continued, "come with us to my home. Our hospitality is the least I can offer for your news."

"My thanks to you and your wife but, regrettably, I must decline. I am expected elsewhere this evening."

"Then it would be wrong of me to delay you. However, please remember that from today, the door of Martin Hydale will always be open to you."

"Again, my thanks to you," Malagar clasped Martin's hand once more. "I hope your daughter returns safely into your arms. Farewell." He nodded to each of the others, then stepped past and hastened away.

Martin turned to his friends. "Come, a meal for you all then I must set off after Martha."

"Yes, but not alone, Martin. I'll be coming with you."

"My thanks, John, but you have a farm, a family."

"And both can survive for a spell without me whilst I help a friend."

"Friends," Peter and Abel added in unison, and Martin knew it was useless to say any more.

"Very well," he conceded instead. And as they walked back towards his cottage, he was not the only one to wonder where the stranger, Malagar, was expected that night, for the next habitation was more than a day’s walk to the south.

## VII

Martha and the cub had stumbled upon a small cave in the Harad foothills as dusk had fallen. After a shared meal of bread, milk, and cheese, they had curled up together to sleep. Without the cave, a mist had sprung up and from this mist crept two shadowy forms. They stole, catlike, towards the cave and the prize that lay within.

The girl would bring a lot of gold if sold in the right market, and these two knew just where to find a buyer. It was a sickening trade, but money was a powerful sweetener. They crept closer.

Within, Martha slept soundly, but the cub stirred and pricked up its ears. It slipped from Martha's side and slunk into the middle of the cave, its hackles rising.

The two malefactors paused at the cave mouth, listening.

Satisfied that their victim must be asleep, they exchanged a thumbs-up sign and prepared to attack.

A low growl froze them in their tracks. They swapped puzzled glances - the girl’s only companion had been a small dog, a puppy. After a moment’s silence, they exchanged hand signals - the language of thieves - and once more advanced upon their prey.

The low growl came again and this time an enormous shadow accompanied it, detaching itself from the darkness of the cave-mouth and prowling forward on four legs. The would-be slavers stared in terror at the fully grown wolf that stood before them, paled at the sight of its gleaming fangs and razor claws. It padded closer to them and snarled viciously. As one, like birds before a storm, the villains turned and fled.

The wolf stood before the cave for several minutes more. It carefully sniffed the air, satisfying itself that the sleeping girl was safe, then it padded back to her side, resuming the form of a cub as it did so.

## VIII

Next morning, in the hour before dawn, the cubs frolicking awakened Martha. Over a breakfast of milk and sweet cakes, she sat and spoke solemnly with the cub.

"We may find the dragon today. I hope you'll be ready."

The cub cocked its small head and barked once.

"It may be dangerous, so if you don't want to come..."

The cub cut her off with another two barks. It wagged its tail, dashed into her lap, and licked her face.

Laughing at its exuberance, she pushed the cub gently away.

"Okay, we'll go on together and help each other be brave."

The cub wagged its tail as if in approval and licked her face again.

Still laughing, she gathered up her things and they left the cave to begin their climb into the Harad Mountains.

## IX

Less than two miles away, breakfast was also being had in another camp. Martha's father and his companions had ridden hard to arrive at the Varynym forest and pass through it, torches lit and calling Martha's name. A few hours after midnight, they stopped to rest and now, as day broke, hoped to find some trace of Martha and thus a trail to follow.

"Martin! John! Peter!" Abel's cry went up from the northern side of the road, where he had gone to relieve himself.

The others rushed to join him.

"What is it, Abel?" Martin demanded breathlessly.

"Look."

They followed the line of Abel's pointing finger to where a set of small footprints lay in the dirt.

"Martha?" John asked, turning to Martin.

"Yes. Get the pack and horses together and follow me." He rushed off in pursuit of his daughter.

The footprints ran out barely twenty yards on, where the dirt became populated by patches of coarse grass. But by then Martin had the pointer he needed. Martha, for whatever reason, had left the road for the arduous terrain of the Harad foothills. Was she actually heading into the mountains, Martin wondered. She must be, he decided.

"She's headed up into the mountains," he called back to the others.

"The mountains. Why would she go into the mountains?" John wondered aloud.

"I don’t know," Martin shouted back. "But the sooner we find her, the sooner she can explain. Come on!"

Two hours later, they found the cave in which Martha had spent the night. And, to Martin's horror, the tracks of the two men that had crept up upon the cave.

"Whoever they were, Martin, they didn't get into the cave," Peter offered, as Martin's expression darkened. "Their footsteps stop here."

"But," Martin rumbled, "whoever they are, they're still out there and so is my daughter!"

"There are more tracks here," called Abel from above the cave. "They look like Martha's."

Martin hurried to Abel's side. "Yes," he agreed, "and she's still climbing into the mountains."

"Much further up and we won't be able to follow with the horses." Peter was surveying the ground above them.

"Best we leave them here, then. Will you stay with them, Peter?" Martin asked.

Peter nodded. "I'll wait right here till you return."

"All right. We three will keep going on foot."

John and Abel nodded in agreement, then they climbed the ever steeper gradient above the cave.

## X

Martha had given no thought to where in the mountains she would find the dragon. But, as she was climbing, she chanced upon a narrow game trail and had followed that. It wound between boulders and rock-slides, past wild scrub and rough patches of heather, all the while leading her higher and higher. The cub trotted along by her heels, yapping playfully and prancing from side to side of the trail.

"I just don't know where this dragon will be," she told the cub. "Will it live in a cave? Or amongst boulders?" She stopped and looked up at the trail before them. It wove back and fore across a flat, grassy area before winding up a sharp incline and disappearing into a stand of stunted trees.

"I guess we'd better just keep going for now."

The cub barked and dashed ahead a few paces. It turned and looked back at her, tail wagging furiously.

"What are you trying to tell me?" She wished the cub could talk. Instead, it barked again and ran a few more paces.

"All right. If you think you know the way, all I can do is follow." She followed the cub across the plain and up into the trees.

Beyond the trees, a small but steep-sided ridge confronted them.

"I don't think we can climb this. We'll have to find a way around." She turned to her right and looked for another path.

The cub barked behind her.

"What?" She craned her head over her shoulder. The cub was standing a little way off, half-turned in the opposite direction, its tail wagging frantically.

"That way?" She couldn't understand how the cub thought it knew where to find the dragon. Still, she decided she had nothing to lose by taking its advice.

A little over twenty paces along the cub's chosen route, they came upon a break in the ridge - a narrow fissure barely two feet wide. Martha could see daylight through the gap and, as the cub had already dashed inside, she crept through.

On the far side, the rift opened out onto a plateau which was spread with more withered, grey trees like those they had seen below. Martha followed the cub into the forest and as they passed deeper into it, she felt a growing sense of unease.

As the trees thinned out so her anxiety deepened. She felt sure that there was something very close by, something that was aware of her presence.

"The dragon," she breathed, terrified to speak aloud lest it should hear and leap to the attack.

Presently, they passed the last of the trees and found themselves upon the rim of a deep hollow. Lying curled below them, in the middle of the hollow, was the dragon. Martha gasped at the size of it - it was enormous, truly terrible. And suddenly it was moving. Its long, serpentine neck unfolded from its side and its wide, horned head drew level with Martha. Great golden eyes fixed upon her and the creature's nostrils flared.

"Child?" Martha recoiled, amazed. The beast had spoken. "Why do you come before me?"

Dragon's breath swirled around Martha. She gagged. The beast reeked of rotten eggs.

"I say again, child, why do you come before me?"

"Be… be…" Martha stammered.

The dragon's golden eyes flashed red. "Do not fear me, child. Just speak." As terrible a creature as it appeared, the dragon's tone was kindly.

At last Martha found her tongue. "Because what you are doing is wrong. You steal cattle and destroy crops. And you have brought great sorrow to my family and friends. I have come to tell you to stop!"

And now the dragon's eyes flared an iridescent blue. Thunder rumbled deep in its throat. "Stop? How may I stop? I do what I do to survive, it is the way of things."

"But it's wrong!" Martha stamped a foot. "The village is dying! The land is dying! And when the children grow up, there will be nothing for them!" The moisture of tears glistened in Martha's eyes.

Silence fell as the dragon seemed to consider Martha's words. Its eyes flashed a variety of colours - red, blue, green, amber - but always returned to gold.

Martha's tears were flowing freely now, and she did not try to hide or stem them.

"Why do you cry?" the dragon asked her softly.

Martha looked up at the beast. "Because I am sad."

"For your village? Your friends?"

"Yes. But mostly, I'm sad for you." She stepped closer to the dragon and, despite its noisome breath and fearful maw, reached out a hand to stroke its muzzle. At her side, the wolf-cub wagged its tail in enthusiastic approval.

To Martha's surprise, a giant tear formed in the corner of the dragon's right eye. "But why, child, are you sad for me?"

"Because to the entire world you are a monster and nobody sees that you have no choice other than to be as you are."

As Martha said this, the dragon's first tear fell to the ground with a great splash. A second swelled in its left eye and quickly joined its twin upon the earth.

"I am sorry, child." The words were barely audible, but Martha heard and understood. Then, as her tears abated, so the dragon's quickly became a flood. She stepped back as tremendous sobs racked the dragon's body and its tears fell faster. And as she watched the noble beast, she saw it was growing smaller.

"What?" she said in confusion and then she saw. The crater was filling with the dragon's tears and as it filled and the tears fell, so the dragon was diminished. Shocked, she turned away and discovered her father and his companions looking on in wonder. She dashed into her father's arms.

"Oh, Martha," he cried and held her close.

A sudden bark caused them to break their embrace. Martha looked back and saw the wolf-cub standing a few feet away. It wagged its tail, barked again, and looked back to where the crater was now almost full. Then, before Martha could reach its side, it turned and dashed away. As it went, its body lengthened and thickened, its dash became a powerful lope and it slowly faded from view. Martha stared longingly after her erstwhile companion and friend.

"Come on, Martha." Martin Hydale placed a gentle hand on his daughter's shoulder. "You have a wonderful tale to tell."

Martha turned and clasped her father's hand, fresh tears in her eyes. "Yes," she said simply as they began the journey home.

Behind them, a trickle of perfect, clear water broached the rim of the crater. Soon the trickle would become a stream and the stream a river which, in time, would revitalise the ravaged land below.
